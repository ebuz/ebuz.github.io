Research interest

I work on understanding how and why speakers vary their speech. While some of this variation is simply related to motor and neural noise, an open question (and the one I am interested in) is to what extent production variability reflects the underlying preparatory/planning processes that allow us to eloquently produce spontaneous speech and what variability might be tied to effectively communicating information. My more recent work focuses on understanding if and how speech variation is attributable to task-relevant learning/adaptation.

Autobiography

I'm from Los Angeles, California. I graduated from the Johns Hopkins University in 2008 with a BA in Cognitive Science and a minor in Computer Science. I worked on a fun project with Robert Frank on learning of multiple independent hierarchical transformations of linear sequences. After, I worked in the Cognitive Neurology/Neuropsychology lab with Kerry Ledoux on finding implicit measures of verbal comprehension in non-verbal autistic children/adults. I'm now (probably going to be) earning a PhD in Brain and Cognitive Sciences at the University of Rochester, working primarily with Florian Jaeger and more recently Michael Tanenhaus.
